About Feasting in the Great Hall

Feasts in Barley Hall were elaborate social events. Seating arrangements, food choices, and tableware all reflected hierarchy and hospitality. Each detail was meaningful.

Seating & Service

Image: Noble family dining with servants (from Luttrell Psalter)

The lord and honored guests sat at the head table — the higher the status, the closer the seating. Servants brought food and drink from the buttery and pantry through service passages.

Food & Utensils

Flesh hook used to retrieve meat from pots.

Meats like beef, pork, lamb, and poultry were typical, often roasted, boiled, or baked. Guests used knives, spoons, or even trencher bread as plates — forks were rare in this period.

Did you know? During special feasts, peacocks were roasted and presented with feathers carefully arranged — eye-catching but quite tough to eat!

Fish & Fasting Customs

Illustration: Fish served during fasting periods.

During fasts like Lent and on Fridays, fish replaced meat. Freshwater and saltwater fish were both common. Curiously, beaver tails were sometimes classified as “fish” due to their scaly texture!

Pigs & Banquets

Illustration: Pigs, often farmed for common meals.

Pigs were a staple but often seen as lower-class fare. Only the young “suckling pig” was fit for a noble banquet. In one quirky medieval event, a pig was even put on trial and executed for injuring a child!
